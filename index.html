Rooibokkraal
Est. 1953 · Crocodile River Valley · Limpopo

The land on which Rooibokkraal sits has been witness to some of the most significant chapters in South African history — from the first European explorers crossing the Crocodile River, to the great Boer republics, and finally into the hands of the Faber family in 1953. This is that story.

1836
Cornwallis Harris & the First Explorers

Captain William Cornwallis Harris arrived at the Cape in June 1836, invalided from the East India Company with fever. Rather than rest, he organised one of the great early hunting expeditions into the South African interior. His route took him across the Marico River and into the Magaliesberg — the same landscape that borders Rooibokkraal today.

It was on this expedition that Harris discovered the Sable Antelope, later named the Harris Buck in his honour, and produced detailed watercolour paintings that gave the world its first visual record of southern Africa's wildlife. His published account, Wild Sports of Southern Africa (1839), remains a classic of exploration literature.

1834–36
Dr Andrew Smith's Scientific Expedition

At nearly the same time, Dr Andrew Smith — Scottish surgeon, naturalist and founder of the South African Museum — led the Expedition for Exploring Central Africa. His party of scientists travelled north of the Orange River through the Marico region and down towards the Crocodile River, documenting flora, fauna and indigenous peoples with meticulous detail.

Smith's diary was eventually published by the Van Riebeeck Society in 1939. He met a young Charles Darwin in Cape Town in 1836 on Darwin's return from the Beagle voyage, and the two corresponded for years.

1870s
Frederick Selous — The Greatest Hunter

Frederick Courteney Selous — British explorer, hunter and conservationist — made his early reputation in this very region. He first arrived in southern Africa at age 19 and spent years hunting along the Limpopo and Marico systems. Local hunters near Rooibokkraal describe the area as one of the finest hunting grounds Selous ever visited, a reputation that drew adventurers from across the British Empire.

Selous's real-life exploits inspired Sir H. Rider Haggard to create Allan Quatermain — the fictional hero of King Solomon's Mines. He was friends with Theodore Roosevelt and Cecil Rhodes, and in 1893 was awarded the Founder's Medal of the Royal Geographical Society.

1880s
The ZAR, the Boers & the Land Survey

Following the First Boer War, the London Convention of 1884 established the boundaries of the Zuid-Afrikaansche Republiek (ZAR) and settled the Tswana territorial question — with Tswana communities being moved into the Bechuanaland Protectorate. This freed vast tracts of the western Transvaal lowveld for formal survey and sale.

The ZAR Volksraad sold surveyed farm portions to Burghers as reward and settlement. It was in this period that the farms of the Crocodile River valley — including the land that would become Rooibokkraal — were first formally registered.

1887
Van Staaden & the Two Rivers Estate

In 1887, a man named Van Staaden acquired Rooibokkraal — the same year construction began on the Delagoa Bay Railway (the Pretoria–Maputo line), which would transform the lowveld economy. Around this time, the Transvaal and Delagoa Bay Land Company was acquiring multiple farms along the Crocodile River, consolidating them into what became known as the Two Rivers Estate.

1953
EA Faber Buys Rooibokkraal — The Family Begins

In 1953, EA Faber purchased Rooibokkraal Portion 2 from Westerman. His intention was simple and enduring: to give his family a place to come to the bush — to be among the animals, to breathe the bushveld air, to step away from busy lives and reconnect with what matters. A place to disconnect, and to spend real time with family and friends.

It was the beginning of a family connection to this land that now spans over seventy years.

The farm sits in the Crocodile River valley, the same river that Harris and Selous had crossed over a century before, and the same landscape that Smith's scientists had documented in meticulous detail.

1955
Growing the Land

EA Faber purchased Rooibokkraal Portion 1 from Ted Fair, consolidating the two portions of the original farm. In the same year, Krokodilsdraai and Buffelsdraai were bought from Wallace — expanding the family's foothold along the river that gives Krokodilsdraai its name.

1957
Schwerin

The farm Schwerin was purchased from Dolf Erasmus, adding another piece to what was becoming a substantial family holding in the valley.

1959
The Strip

The final piece — known simply as "the Strip" — was acquired in 1959, completing the assemblage of land that the family has stewarded ever since.

Today
Four Generations, One Place

EA Faber's vision has held. Over seventy years on, the land remains in family hands — and the reason people come is still exactly what he intended. The bushveld. The animals. The silence. A fire at night, a sundowner at dusk, the kind of unhurried time that ordinary life rarely allows.

Three families — Faber, Charter and Jones — gather here across the seasons. Children who grew up on this land now bring their own children. The stories told around the fire are layered with decades of memory: the animals seen, the floods endured, the braais that lasted till dawn.

Rooibokkraal is not just a farm. It is the place the family returns to — the constant in a changing world, held together by the quiet understanding that some things are worth keeping.
